§00

Why loops, not prompts

A single model call cannot carry a multi-step objective: it cannot observe its own results, recover from failure, or hand work forward. Loop engineering is the discipline of wrapping agents in iteration cycles with objective verifiers, narrow feedback, explicit budgets, and verifier-gated exits.

Pursuit is EphemeralOS's loop runtime: a durable, caller-agnostic orchestrator that hard-codes two well-engineered loops — retry inside a leg and relay across legs — and exposes the interfaces an outer operator loop needs. Loop control is fixed and race-hardened in the service; loop policy (what to verify, when to refocus, what to defer) belongs to planners, prompts, and user-authored context scripts.

Sophisticated control, simple policy — never the other way around.
§01

The loop stack

Three loops, three owners, three verifiers. Each loop lives at the level that owns the goal representation its exit condition is judged against.

FIG.1 — Nested loop orbitstokens orbit at loop cadence

Reading the figure: inner loops spin faster. The retry token completes many circuits per relay circuit; the operator token moves slowest of all — it only acts at pursuit settlement. The work-item DAG at center is not a loop: it executes once per attempt, hard-gated by depends_on.

Each loop lives where its goal representation lives — that is the only place its exit can be judged.

From inside, a pursuit is a one-shot mission: nothing in the service ever re-launches a pursuit. The outer iteration exists only in the operator's context — deliberately, because a pursuit can succeed at its stated goal while missing what the operator actually wanted, and only the intent-holder can detect that.

§02

Anatomy & vocabulary

Five entities. Goal text is immutable at the top, effective-with-provenance in the middle, and disposable at the bottom. The vocabulary is deliberate: legs advance, attempts retry in place — the old workflow/iteration words obscured exactly that distinction.

§03

The relay — legs & the baton

A pursuit advances in legs. The baton is next_leg_goal: a goal string declared by the planner, passed forward only when the current leg closes Success. No successor → the pursuit itself closes Success.

The axes: attempts extend horizontally inside a leg — the retry axis, bounded at max_attempts = 6 (deliberately lenient: a leg that cannot close in six attempts is signaling an unworkable task). Legs extend vertically — the relay axis, unbounded. The spawn rule: leg_n+1 is created only at the instant leg_n closes Success with a standing next_leg_goal; before that moment no leg_n+1 row, directory, or goal exists anywhere. In dynamic mode the newest leg always carries the goal that would close the pursuit — each planner decides whether that is too much for its own leg and defers the remainder downward.

Why "relay" is the right metaphor (both modes): each leg gets fresh runners (new agent contexts — no context-window exhaustion across a long pursuit), the baton is small (a prose goal, not accumulated history), and the handoff is gated (success means the full effective leg goal, never "goal minus deferral"). The modes differ only in who owns the route: dynamic legs are discovered by planners; predefined legs are caller checkpoints known upfront — which is why all three exist from creation in FIG.3.

The success invariant

Deferral happens at planning time, never at close time. A planner that cannot achieve the full leg_goal must narrow it (refocus) and peel the remainder into next_leg_goal — before the work runs. By the time the closing attempt succeeds, success is unambiguous:

Success means the full effective leg_goal was achieved.
Success never means "leg_goal minus next_leg_goal".

Two leg-goal modes

Contract subtlety: in dynamic mode there is no payload shape for clearing a standing next_leg_goal alone. Clearing successor scope is a refocus act — declare the replacement leg_goal and omit next_leg_goal in the same payload.

§04

Adaptive retry — attempts inside a leg

The leg is the loop frame: it owns the goal, the budget, and the exits. Attempts are its iterations. A retry is not a re-run — the retry planner receives structured failure_reasons, sees only focus-consistent attempts, and may depends_on the previous attempt's still-successful work.

FIG.4 — One leg, three attemptsloop · 16s
attempts 1/32/33/3· max_attempts:3
attempt_1 planning running failed
planner declares work graph · workers execute DAG
failure_reasons: [ work_item_tests [Failed]: assertion drift in fixtures ]
attempt_2 replanning running failed
retry planner reads failure_reasons — re-plans around the break
depends_on: attempt_1.work_item_schema [Success] · surviving work is reused, not redone
attempt_3 replanning running success
all work items terminal Success — verifier items included
leg closes success → baton check (next_leg_goal?)

The adaptive part: cross-attempt depends_on (same leg, same non-superseded leg_goal_version) means a retry can be "fix one item and re-verify" instead of "redo everything". That is the difference between retry and adaptive retry.

Replan has two grades — and the second one is priced

Re-planning work under the same goal is free; re-planning the goal itself (refocus) supersedes prior attempts and increments leg_goal_version — but the attempt budget does not reset. A pivoting planner cannot disguise flailing as progress; the honest path for genuinely new scope is next_leg_goal.

Derived, not mutated: "superseded" is a pure view — an attempt is superseded iff a later plan in its leg declared leg_goal. Nothing archives at mutation time, so cancel races and idempotent replays cannot corrupt the archive.

§05

The scheduler — inside one attempt

Work items form a DAG over depends_on. One invariant is physics, not policy: a work item launches only when every direct dependency is terminal Success — enforced in the claim query, the post-commit launch guard, and the stale-claim recheck.

FIG.6 — Failure propagationloop · 16s
reconcileAttemptStatus: no Running, no NotStarted → close attempt → FAILED failure_reasons: work_item_D [Blocked]: blocked by work_item_C failure_reasons: work_item_C [Failed]: <worker summary first line>

Watch item B: C's failure does not touch it. Unrelated running work continues; unrelated pending work still launches when its own dependencies succeed. The attempt closes Failed only after block propagation leaves nothing Running or NotStarted — so retries begin at true exhaustion, never early.

Scheduler invariants (physics)

  • Hard launch gate. Claimable ⇔ NotStarted ∧ attempt Running ∧ every direct dependency Success. No optimistic starts.
  • Running never becomes Blocked. If a dependency fails, the dependent was provably never launched. A Running item with a non-Success dependency is state corruption to surface, not repair.
  • Propagation runs to fixpoint. A fails → B Blocked → C (depends on B) Blocked. Transitive chains close in one settlement pass.
  • Failure is a list. failure_reasons[] records every failed and blocked item — the retry planner sees the whole break surface, not the first symptom.
§06

Verification is planned, not hardcoded

The framework does not impose a verification gate. The planner owns it: assign verifier work items that depends_on the work they check. The verify-fix loop then rides the existing retry machinery for free — bounded by the same budget.

verify_a submits Failed → attempt closes Failed → its findings travel through failure_reasons → the retry planner re-plans: fix + re-verify, reusing surviving items via cross-attempt depends_on.

The one non-negotiable

A verifier's verdict rides its STATUS, not its prose.
// Findings written into a Success outcome are a verification PASS —
// the attempt closes Success and the defects flow forward as achievement.
If verification fails → submit a Failed outcome. Always.

Where this policy lives: the planner directive in .eos-agents/pursuit/scripts/planner.cjs ("end the DAG with a validator") and the verifier profile's worker directive. Scripts are user-authored per deployment — each repo sets its own verification floor without touching @eos/pursuit. The floor is worker self-assessment; the ceiling is whatever verification the planner designs.

§07

The operator — the outer loop

The main agent supervises at the boundary; it never drives an inner iteration. Once launched, the pursuit loops autonomously — durable, DB-guarded, background. The operator's touchpoints are exactly an operator's:

01 · Launch
delegate_pursuit(pursuit_goal) — or createPursuit(...) for non-agent callers. One open pursuit per supervisor.
02 · Await
settle() resolves at terminal Success / Failed / Cancelled. No polling of internals.
03 · Judge
Read outcome.md — the ordered leg ledger — against intent, not against the goal string.
04 · Decide
Met → done. Not met → re-launch with an amended pursuit_goal. Runaway → cancel_background_session.
FIG.8 — The operator loop · a harness while-loop over tool callsloop · 22s
while not submit_main_outcome(…): # the loop sits ABOVE the agent — the harness re-invokes each turn; the agent never self-loops
1tool_callbash · read · edit — gather ground truth, scope the ask
·thinkframe the intent as one verifiable pursuit_goal
2tool_calldelegate_pursuit(pursuit_goal)⏳ awaiting settle() — legs & attempts sealed inside
⟵ settle(): Failed · outcome.md — leg_2 attempt budget exhausted · failure_reasons[]
·thinkreview outcome vs intent → not met · amend the goal with what the failure taught
3tool_callbash · read — inspect pursuit_1’s persisted context store
4tool_calldelegate_pursuit(pursuit_goal′)⏳ awaiting settle()
⟵ settle(): Success · outcome.md — all legs success (3 legs, dynamically spawned)
·thinkverify against intent → met
5tool_callsubmit_main_outcome(success: true, summary) — terminates the loop
→ condition met · harness stops re-invoking · operator loop exits

The while-loop belongs to the harness, not the model. The main agent never self-loops: each turn the harness re-invokes it, the agent emits think + tool_call, the harness executes and appends the result, and the cycle repeats until the agent terminates it with submit_main_outcome. At this altitude delegate_pursuit is just another tool call beside bash and edit — an unusually long-running one that returns a verifiable settlement. The retry-until-met behavior (review → amend → re-delegate) is semantics the agent enacts inside the sequence, not harness control flow; the inner relay and retry loops (FIG.2–7) run entirely behind each delegate_pursuit call.

This makes the operator the system's actual verification layer: attempt success ultimately rests on worker- and verifier-claimed settlements, so the independent judgment that closes the loop objectively lives with whoever holds the intent. Pursuit supplies that judge the two hard ingredients — a structured, inspectable outcome and a clean terminal handle — and deliberately supplies no outer-loop machinery.

Continuity: the context store

A re-launched pursuit starts as a fresh mission, but the system is not amnesiac: every pursuit's full context tree — pursuit → leg → attempt → plan → work items, one fact per .md file — persists on disk under .eos-agents/pursuit/context/pursuit_<id>/ and outlives settlement. The operator folds its judgment into the next pursuit_goal, and context-exploration tools (planned) will let a successor pursuit's agents read predecessor trees directly rather than relying on goal prose alone. Within a leg, memory is already first-class: a retry planner shares context across attempts by declaring depends_on against work items of prior attempts that remain is_consistent_with_leg_goal.

Loop-engineering scorecard

The five components every engineered loop needs, scored per level. Green cells are service-guaranteed; amber are policy (prompts/scripts); cyan live in the operator.

ComponentL2 · Retry (leg)L1 · Relay (pursuit)L0 · Operator (main agent)
Clear goal leg_goal; success = all items Success goal chain via declared next_leg_goal pursuit_goal — "met" judged vs intent
Termination max_attempts = 6 (lenient); refocus can't refresh it — the bounded horizontal axis no successor → Success; exhausted leg → Failed; leg count unbounded (vertical axis) none provided — caller must self-limit
Feedback / context failure_reasons[]; consistent attempts only; cross-attempt depends_on next planner gets prior leg outcomes persisted context store on disk; exploration tools planned; operator amends the goal
Error handling block propagation, death & compose synthesis leg failure escalates; cancel cascades operator judgment
Verification planned — verifier work items; verdict-via-status success-gated baton pass independent outcome review

Width, lanes, budgets, memory — where each one lives

  • Exploration is horizontal, inside the attempt. A planner can fan out many exploration work items and gate a collection/synthesis item on them with depends_on — "run N approaches, synthesize the winner" is one plan. Workers carry their own subagents for depth. Parallel exploration lives in the work graph, not in extra leg lanes; planners are expected to be powerful and plan wide.
  • One baton per leg — intentional lane discipline. Attempt-level fan-out is already dynamic multi-baton, so legs stay single-lane: the pursuit never crosses too many lanes at once. How many legs exist is the agent's decision — in dynamic mode the newest leg always carries the goal that would close the pursuit, and its planner decides whether that is too much for one leg and defers the remainder.
  • Bounded horizontally, unbounded vertically. max_attempts (currently 6) bounds retries within a leg — deliberately lenient: a leg that cannot close in six attempts is signaling an unworkable task, not an underfunded one. The leg chain itself has no cap.
  • Memory is the context store. The pursuit → leg → attempt → plan → work-item tree persists to disk as per-field .md files; cross-attempt sharing rides depends_on against is_consistent_with_leg_goal attempts; context-exploration tools are planned for read access across the store (§07).
Fixed loop semantics are what make the invariants provable and the test matrix finite.
